Senior Health Portfolio Artifact 4

Eating / Image Disorders


Focus Question:
2.1.12.C.1: After evaluating risk factors, how susceptible is a person to having an eating disorder?

To answer this question I found interviews with counsellors that included the professional insights they have about risk factors and susceptibility.

Personal Connection:
I feel connected to this topic because my cousin has an eating disorder. She has struggled with anorexia for over 5 years.

Artifact:
The authors of the two interviews are Margarita Tartakovsky, M.S. and Rebecca Aponte.

Sari Fine Shepphird, PhD, is a clinical psychologist and eating disorder specialist. One of the questions she was asked was specifically about the warning signs for eating disorders and also what underlying issues an eating disorder may often be a symptom of. Shepphird Interview

Robin S. Rosenberg, PhD, is a member of the Academy for Eating Disorders and a Fellow of the American Academy of Clinical Psychology. She was interviewed about treating eating disorders but also talks about risk factors and susceptibility. Rosenberg Interview

Summary:
Some common warning signs for eating disorders are:

  • the person avoids eating with others;

  • starts to restrict the types of foods they eat as well as the quantity;

  • becomes secretive about their eating;

  • skips meals;

  • makes frequent trips to the bathroom after meals;

  • starts to exercise excessively.

Some more subtle warning signs for eating disorders are:

  • the person starts to dress in very baggy clothes;

  • shows great concern about weight gain and makes a lot of weight-related comments;

  • loses or gains a significant amount of weight;

  • develops perfectionistic tendencies;

  • shows signs of low self-esteem;

  • is embarrassed or ashamed after eating.

There are various risk factors for eating disorders:

  • Both doctors in the interviews agree that in our culture, the ideal body is basically unattainable, unless you spend all your time at it or have plastic surgery.

  • Young women are exposed to many media and role models who fuel social pressure to be thin.

  • A family preoccupation with weight, food, and appearance can become internalized.

  • Children should not be given restricted diets. Natural hunger and fullness cues can be affected if children are made to feel guilty for what they eat.

  • Older women also face unrealistic pressures about their image and appearance. Challenges such as divorce, childbirth, and menopause are events that can trigger anorexia at a later age.

  • Male athletes have a higher reported rate of eating disorders, partly due to the belief that weight loss is necessary for peak performance.

  • Homosexual and bisexual males also have a higher rate of anorexia.

More risk factors for eating disorders:

  • People with eating disorders often report having been teased about their bodies or appearance .

  • People with eating disorders often are experiencing underlying emotional distress, relationship or psychological conflicts, past trauma, or difficult life transitions.

  • For some people, anorexia is a distraction from other painful feeling or life events they feel they can't manage.

Analysis:
Based on the risk factors that the doctors in the two interviews talk about, I think it would be very difficult to predict how susceptible a person is to having an eating disorder. I think that most of us are exposed to some level of the pressures that are described, and many of us are also dealing with problems in our lives.

Maybe the image pressures would have to be very extreme. Maybe with teasing or other put downs put on top of the image pressures, and with very difficult conflicts and painful situations in the person's life, an eating disorder is like their breaking point. There are differences in how resilient different people are. Maybe that is a key aspect of how susceptible a person is to having an eating disorder.
